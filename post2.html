Cybercrime Group Steals $1.3M from Banks

A cybercriminal group dubbed the Florentine Banker Group launched advanced business email compromise (BEC) attacks on leading Israeli and UK financial firms, stealing $1.3 million dollars in mere four separate transactions. The Florentine Banker team stole email credentials and lurked for 2 months before diverting important wire transfers worth millions of dollars, a brand - new report by Check Point Research shows. $600,000 was recovered through some emergency intervention immediately after the attack. “People must understand that these attacks happen fast. In most instances, once the wire transfers are made, they’re lost for good; our clients were lucky to even get half their money back”. Says Lotem Finkelstein, Check Point's threat intelligence group head. Check Point had been watching the activities of The Florentine Banker Group for a minimum of six months before his firm started working with the victims last December. The targeted companies are three large UK and Israeli financial sector firms that weekly transfer large sums (in the numerous dollars) to new partners and third-party providers. Phishing emails targeting two top officials were sent over number of weeks, and sometimes added new people to the list of targets until they gained access to victim’s email account. Once the attackers gained access, they were able to read through the emails to know the various channels utilised by the victim to transfer money. The next move was to isolate the victim from third parties and internal colleagues by creating malicious mailbox rules. During this case, emails with pre-defined words such as "invoice," "returned," or "fail," would be moved to another folder not commonly utilised by the victim, like the RSS folder. Head of world threat intelligence, Richard Henderson, says that at this time the attacker has total visibility and control over the account. They need to think about the human factor which incorporates training the staff on a way to spot bad links and fraudulent emails, and fixing processes where the people accountable for large wire transfers add a second verification factor by either calling one that asked to form the transfer, or calling the receiving party. Companies must over-pass their top people and educate them on the requirement for multifactor authentication.
