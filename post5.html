How dark web fuels insider threats

New decentralized, criminal marketplaces and "as-a-service" offerings make it easy for workers to monetize their knowledge and access to enterprise networks and systems. Most enterprises give some thought to the Dark Web as an infinite marketplace for cybercrime — exploits, hacking services, stolen data marketplaces, and so on. while this can be often, there are other elements which can not be so apparent but are equally problematic for enterprises: Malicious insiders trying to seek out a simple because of monetize their knowledge and access. Two simultaneous trends are opening new areas of profound risk to enterprises via the Dark We – It’s becoming both decentralized and democratized. The two movements that are converging to make true problems are as follows:  Instead of seeing various marketplaces today, which can be taken down by enforcement with meaningful impact, you instead see the equivalent of fair booths where threat actors sell their wares as independent vendors.  Today, people with limited technical skills can simply subscribe services that will perform their malfeasance for them. From an insider-threat perspective, this trend has also made it easy for insiders to monetize their knowledge and access, just by participating within the various "as-a-service" offerings on the Dark Web or by directly promoting access to networks and systems. Just within the previous couple of weeks, our researchers have seen several insider-related offerings that should resonate with many companies. These include:  Insider-trading-as-a-service sites. These are relatively common over the previous couple of years. Typically, for a fee, subscribers get access to a delicate stream of data. The foremost recent site we found offers one-time tips for $500 or an annual subscription for one bitcoin, currently valued north of $7,000. Trading is often thought of as a white-collar crime executed by superior executives and financiers. However, when one considers what percentage staff members within the everyday enterprise have access to non-public material information (legal, accounting, investor relations, IT, marketing, sales, etc.), the addressable marketplace for this sort of site — both people selling their information and other people functioning on it — is admittedly big.  Access to a live travel database with many master card credentials. Mostly likely an IT professional, offered access to the customer database of an internet travel site. Unlike typical data breach dumps where the info can go stale over time, during this case, the person was offering live access to the database. In theory, bad actors could siphon master card credentials indefinitely (at least until the insider gets caught). Access was being sold for 1,500 euros.  Access to a hedge fund trading algorithm. This was a remarkable one. An insider at a hedge fund was selling access to the company's "crown jewel" trading algorithm for $300,000, advertising how it had beaten the marketplace for several years. Subscribers would receive credentials to achieve access to the algorithm, and so they may do their own research or just mimic the hedge fund's investing strategy to reap the identical benefits as its clients (without the management fees, of course). This is often a unique reasonably material possession theft — not "stealing" anything, per se, but instead, employing a company's proprietary technology for private gain. In each of those cases, security technology is of limited use in catching the threat actors because there’s no technology which will stop someone from walking out the door with information in their head or system credentials scribbled on a bit of paper. Using the intelligence data as evidence, the enterprise security and fraud teams pinpoint the systems that were accessed, correlate access with access management logs, and narrow down the potential suspects. All of this leads us to the final word irony of today's Dark Web — because the new trends of decentralization and democratization make it significantly more dangerous to enterprises, the most effective countermeasure isn't fancy new technology; it’s human intelligence accelerated by technology.
